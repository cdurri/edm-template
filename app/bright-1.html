First H2H data: U300 glargine vs U100 degludec View online
In BRIGHT, U300 glargine demonstrated:
  • powerful HbA1c reduction*
  • lower rates and incidence of anytime† hypoglycaemia during the active titration period*
  • Comparable incidence and rates of anytime† hypoglycaemia thereafter*
when compared with U100 degludec in insulin-naïve patients with uncontrolled T2DM.1
*Not the primary endpoint
Primary endpoint: Effective HbA1c reduction of -1.6% with U300 glargine, non-inferior vs U100 degludec, ITT population.
HbA1c change: LS mean difference vs U100 degludec: -0.05%; 95% CI -0.15 to 0.05; p<0.0001 for non-inferiority of U300 glargine vs U100 degludec.

Why hypoglycaemia matters
Hypoglycaemia is the most common and concerning adverse effect of diabetes medication.2
It matters because:
  • T2DM patients suffering severe hypoglycaemia have almost 3 times increased risk of cardiovascular death3
  • nocturnal hypoglycaemic events reduce sleep quality, and 7 out of 10 lead to daytime tiredness4
  • it makes 61% of family members worry about their loved one.5
Hypoglycaemia or a fear of hypoglycaemia may also have negative effects on:
  • initiation of insulin therapy5
  • effective insulin titration6
  • patient confidence7
  • treatment experience and compliance8
  • glycaemic control.2,6-8
BRIGHT study design
BRIGHT is the first head-to-head study comparing the efficacy and safety of two second-generation basal insulin analogues, offering a valuable insight into the similarity in glycaemic control and difference in hypoglycaemia risk associated with U300 glargine and U100 degludec.
BRIGHTwas a 24-week randomised, multicentre, open-label non-inferiority study following 929 insulin-naïve patients with T2DM uncontrolled by OADs with or without GLP-1 receptor agonists. Patients from 16 countries (158 study sites) were randomised 1:1 to receive an evening dose of U300 glargine (n=466) or U100 degludec (n=463). Achievement of the fasting self-monitored plasma glucose target was aimed for during the active titration period (weeks 0–12), but dose titrations were still allowed thereafter.
CI, confidence interval; GLP-1, glucagon-like peptide-1; H2H, head-to-head; ITT, intent-to-treat; LS, least-squares; OAD, oral anti-hyperglycaemic drug; RR, rate ratio; T2DM, type 2 diabetes mellitus.
